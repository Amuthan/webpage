    Information for prospective students

    Note: The information below is intended for students who wish to work in my group only. I'm putting it out here to help you, the prospective student, to make an informed decision to either join or not join my group.

    The research in my group is broadly about understanding the physics of material behavior across various scales. Depending on the nature of the project, it can either be mathematics-intensive, or computation-intensive, or both. But before you commence your research in my group formally you need to be aware of the following guidelines:

    • Scientific spirit: I expect you to fully assimilate the scientific spirit, understand the way scientific research is conducted and strive to embody the scientific spirit in your research. If you don't care strongly about the scientific spirit, then I don't think you should be doing a Ph.D. in the first place. You can find a very nice and simple introduction to the nature of the science in this link.

    • Research ethics and integrity: It is imperative that you hold yourself to the highest ethical standards when it comes to research. Please read the guidelines given in this link to understand certain basic aspects of research ethics. Scientific integrity is an indispensable to doing good research; please be advised that there will be severe consequences in line with the institute policies if I find any evidence that you engaged in any form of academic misconduct.

    • Growth mindset: Pursuing a Ph.D. is a serious time commitment from both your and my end. It is very probable that you may feel overwhelmed, overworked, or lost during the process. That is natural, and it is totally okay to feel like that. What is really important is that you learn how to cope with failures of various kinds, which are inevitable in your scientific journey. Rather than being dejected by failure, it really helps to embrace them as opportunities to re-evaluate the situation and improve yourself. If you join my group, you are always welcome to talk to me about any difficulty you may be facing. It is important for me that you feel welcome and find the atmosphere in the research group nurturing so that you may pursue your research to the best of your abilities. But I can't help you if you don't talk to me. While on this topic, I highly recommend reading Mindset: the new psychology of success by Carol Dweck.

    • Work hard: Doing a Ph.D. in my group will definitely involve a lot of hard work. I can assure you that it will be quite challenging, but it will be well worth the effort in the end. My goal is to help you become a good and independent researcher by the time you graduate. If you don't like to work hard, I don't think you should join my group. I will, of course, make sure that you have the necessary resources and help to ensure that you enjoy the process, but there are no shortcuts in research: you need to work hard and you need to learn how to persist in the face of failures.

    In addition to the general guidelines mentioned above, you are also expected to take care of the following prerequisites on your own. You can, of course, talk to me if you need help with any of these. You are expected to learn the following by the end of the first semester of your Ph.D.:

    • Scientific writing: The ability to write well and to express your ideas clearly is an integral part of doing good science. These skills do not sprout magically. They are rather acquired through deliberate effort and practice. You may find this resource to be a useful starting point. I will give you specific writing and presentation tasks over the course of your Ph.D. to help you develop these skills and learn the art of effective communication.

    • LaTeX: The de-facto standard in preparing technical documents in science and engineering is the LaTeX typesetting system. You will be expected to turn in numerous reports over the course of your Ph.D., and, of course, communicate your reserach to other researchers in the form of peer-reviewed publications. Learning LaTeX will turn out to be very useful in this process. I expect you to learn how to create documents using LaTeX; you may consult this tutorial as a simple starting point. You can also use LaTeX to create presentations; see this tutorial to get started with using the Beamer package.

    • Programming: I expect you have a hands-on knowledge of programming in Python irrespective of whether your project is theoretical or computational. A wonderful resource for learning Python is the video lecture series by Prof. Prabhu Ramachandran, who is a faculty member in the Aerospace Engineering department here at IIT Bombay. You are expected to know the material covered in the videos in the Basic-Programming-using-Python folder in the link give earlier. If your Ph.D. research is computational in nature, I expect you to know the basics of programming in C++, in addition to Python. A good tutorial for learning C++ can be found here.

    • Plotting and visualization: Creating good plots and presenting data using effective visualization tools are an integral part of scientific communication. For quick and dirty plotting, I recommend learning Gnuplot; a tutorial can be found here. For creating publication quality plots, I recommend using PyPlot; you can find a good tutorial here.

    • Scientific illustrations: Drawing good schematics provides a very elegant means to communicate various ideas and concepts in your scientific research. It is therefore important to learn how to create good illustrations. I recommend learning Ipe, which is a free and easy-to-use software for creating scientific illustrations. If you are more adventurous, you may optionally try learning the Tikz package; a basic set of tutorials can be found here.

    You will be doing a variety of coursework during the first year of your Ph.D. I will recommend various courses based on the topic of your research. Irrespective of what you actual research is on, I expect you to know the following by the end of the first year of Ph.D.:

    • Basic engineering mathematics: You are supposed to know most of this from your undergraduate and/or masters studies. In any case, a good set of notes that will help you refresh important ideas from basic engineering mathematics can be found here.

    • Classical mechanics: A thorough knowledge of classical mechanics is an essential prerequisite for understanding solid mechanics. This is a non-standard view; be that as it may, I expect you to be proficient in the basic concepts and techniques of classical mechanics. Besides being a very beautiful branch of mathematical physics, classical mechanics is so rich that it was, and continues to be, a wellspring of several key ideas in mathematics, physics and engineering. I highly recommend reading this book to learn classical mechanics.

    These will be guided self-study projects; you are expected to update me weekly on your progress. You are, of course, welcome to discuss these topics with me anytime. In the second year of your Ph.D., after you have completed the necessary coursework and your first APS (Annual Progress Seminar, when you will formally commit to a Ph.D. research topic and formulate a rough plan for your thesis), you are expected to do the following guided self-study, alongside your research:

    • Solid mechanics: Needless to say, you are expected to know the basics of the mechanics of solids. You might have studied various aspects of this during your undergraduate and/or masters courses, but it is important that you get a birds-eye perspective of various aspects of solid mechanics by the end of your second year. A very nice and free online resource can be found here.

    Depending on your actual area of research, I may recommend additional reading material that will be useful for your research. But the three topics mentioned above are mandatory for all students who are interested in working with me. It is not as difficult as it appears, but even if it is, you will eventually find out that it benefits you immensely as you move forward with your Ph.D.

    If you're still game, you are welcome to join my group :)
